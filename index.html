Abstract

Bacterial meningitis is a life-threatening infection affecting the meninges surrounding the brain and spinal cord. This study employed bioinformatics approaches including network pharmacology, KEGG pathway enrichment analysis, and molecular docking to identify potential therapeutic targets associated with bacterial meningitis. Disease-related genes were analyzed, signaling pathways were investigated, and selected compounds were evaluated through docking studies. The results highlighted important molecular targets and demonstrated favorable ligand-protein interactions, providing valuable insights for future drug discovery and therapeutic development.

Objectives

  • To identify genes associated with bacterial meningitis.
  • To construct disease-target interaction networks.
  • To perform pathway enrichment analysis using KEGG.
  • To evaluate ligand-protein interactions through molecular docking.

Introduction

Bacterial meningitis is a severe infection that causes inflammation of the meninges surrounding the brain and spinal cord. Major causative organisms include Neisseria meningitidis, Streptococcus pneumoniae and Haemophilus influenzae. The disease may result in neurological complications, hearing loss, seizures and death if not treated promptly.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Fever
  • Headache
  • Neck stiffness
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Photophobia
  • Confusion
  • Seizures

Risk Factors

  • Weak immune system
  • Crowded living conditions
  • Lack of vaccination
  • Respiratory infections
  • Young age and old age

History of Bacterial Meningitis

Bacterial meningitis has been recognized as a serious neurological disease since the early nineteenth century. The first major epidemics were reported in Europe and North America, where the disease caused significant mortality. Advances in microbiology during the late nineteenth century enabled scientists to identify bacterial pathogens responsible for meningitis.

The development of antibiotics and vaccination programs dramatically reduced disease incidence and mortality. However, bacterial meningitis remains a major public health challenge in developing countries and continues to cause severe neurological complications worldwide.

Pathophysiology of Bacterial Meningitis

The disease begins when pathogenic bacteria enter the bloodstream and cross the blood-brain barrier. Once inside the cerebrospinal fluid, bacteria rapidly multiply and trigger a strong inflammatory response.

Inflammatory mediators increase vascular permeability, resulting in cerebral edema, neuronal injury, and increased intracranial pressure. These pathological changes contribute to neurological complications such as hearing loss, cognitive impairment, seizures, and death.

Literature Review

Thigpen et al. (2011) reported a significant reduction in bacterial meningitis incidence following vaccination programs and highlighted changes in disease epidemiology in the United States.

van de Beek et al. (2004) identified important clinical factors associated with mortality and neurological complications in bacterial meningitis patients.

Brouwer et al. (2012) reviewed the epidemiology, diagnosis and treatment strategies of acute bacterial meningitis and emphasized the importance of early intervention.

Koedel et al. (2010) described the molecular mechanisms responsible for inflammation and neuronal damage during bacterial meningitis.

McGill et al. (2016) provided updated recommendations for the diagnosis and management of bacterial meningitis in adults.

Network Pharmacology Analysis

Network pharmacology analysis was performed to investigate the interactions between disease-associated genes and therapeutic compounds. The analysis helps identify hub genes that play important roles in disease progression and drug response.

Important genes identified in the network included JAK1, STAT3, AKT1, MAPK1, GSK3B, ACE, ROCK2, and ITGB3. These genes participate in immune regulation, inflammatory responses, and signaling pathways associated with bacterial meningitis.

KEGG Pathway Analysis

KEGG pathway enrichment analysis identified important biological pathways associated with bacterial meningitis target genes and signaling mechanisms.

KEGG pathway enrichment analysis identified several significant pathways involved in bacterial meningitis pathogenesis. Major pathways included PI3K-Akt signaling, JAK-STAT signaling, MAPK signaling, Neuroactive ligand-receptor interaction, and cAMP signaling pathways.

These pathways regulate immune responses, inflammatory processes, cellular survival, and communication mechanisms. Understanding these pathways provides insight into disease progression and potential therapeutic targets.
